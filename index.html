About

I'm a Master student in Astrophysics and Theoretical Physics. My main area of interest is Numerics, I developing a PiC simulation in general geometry to solve Vlasov-Maxwell equations to sumulate what happens to plasmas around compact objects like Black Holes or Neutron Stars, my goal is to understand better magnetic reconnection phenomena in the surrounding og these exotic objects. I'm Creative and laborious, easily fit into a team. Truly fascinated by the multi-disciplinary approach to research.

Career

I am currently pursuing a Master’s degree in Astrophysics at the University of Turin and I plan to graduate in October 2020. Since March I have started an Erasmus Traineeship at KU Leuven to do the Thesis, right now I am working from home supervised by Prof. Giovanni Lapenta because of the lockdown.

I chose to carry out my thesis work in the Centre for mathematical Plasma-Astrophysics group at the end of my previous Erasmus program, also in KU Leuven. During the class of Prof. Lapenta on Plasma Dynamics, I coded an explicit Particle-in-Cell (PiC) simulation 1d-3V studying the Weibel and the Filamentation instability, in Newtonian and Relativistic framework. The combined use of computational power and reliable numerical code used to tackle Astrophysical problems really intrigues me.

I have proven to be able to work efficiently and productively in a team, thanks to my Erasmus exchanges. I am a creative hard-worker, interested in multi-disciplinary approach to research. Currently I am developing a module of the massive implicit Particle-in-Cell code iPIC3D for Space Weather Applications. My area of interest is the study of magnetic flux tube instability in general geometry. I am focused on expanding my computational knowledge, diving deep into Numerical Methods and Machine Learning. My passion for Artificial Intelligence and Scientific Coding goes beyond the university path. I co-founded the Machine Learning Journal Club, an open source community based in Turin, now becoming a cultural association, which aims to spread Machine Learning techniques and scientific programming through the university network.

Research

My research focusses on the foundational aspects of cosmology, such as the nature of dark matter, dark energy, gravity and inflation. I mainly work on testing cosmological models with experiments and observational data, such as from—but not only—weak lensing and galaxy clustering surveys. My current effort aims at finding novel observables or combinations of known observables to exploit the large-scale structure information at its best. At the moment, I pursue three different projects with this multi-wavelength, synergistic approach:

  1. The cross-correlation between gamma-ray anisotropies and direct gravitational probes of dark matter (e.g. clustering or lensing), to detect dark matter particles.
  2. The multi-tracer technique, to overcome cosmic variance and detect primordial non-Gaussianity and relativistic effects on scales near and beyond the cosmological horizon.
  3. The use of radio cosmic shear in combination with traditional optical/near-IR weak lensing, to mitigate systematic effects and the spurious contamination from intrinsic alignments.

I am an active member of various international collaborations, like the European Space Agency's Euclid satellite, the Square Kilometre Array (SKA). In the Euclid Theory Working Group, I coordinate the `Dark Matter & Particle Cosmology' Working Package, besides being part of the Editorial Board. For the SKA Cosmology Working Group, I coordinated the `Cosmology on the Largest Scales' Science Case, and I now co-lead the `Synergies' Focus Group. I am also part of: the EMU radio continuum survey collaboration, within whose Cosmology Working Group I am one of the corresponding authors for its forthcoming first science review; the European Space Agency's gravitational-wave space-based mission LISA; the future cosmic microwave background mission COrE+; and the SuperCLASS collaboration, which will soon deliver state-of-the-art radio cosmic shear data.
